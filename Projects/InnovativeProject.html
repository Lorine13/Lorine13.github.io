Technical Aspects

  • Global description

    The objective of the project “Localisation and Communication” is to provide a tool to make the coordination of a task force in a large outdoor space easier. Our project is more precisely targeting groups composed of one operation’s manager (a chief) and a team of operators. Equipped with an electronic dispositive (called a gateway - GW) and a digital tablet, the chief can determine the precise location of each of his operators on a map. In addition, it is possible for him to send messages and interact either with all of his team or just with one specific operator. As for the operators, they are all equipped with another electronic dispositive (called a node - ND) and a mobile phone with which they can both visualise the chief’s location and send him messages.

    Figure 1: Global representation of the system



    The project can be divided in several parts:

    Communication protocols:

    Two communication protocols have been used during this project. We chose the LoRa protocol regarding the communication between the nodes and the gateway. Indeed, LoRa technically offers the four kilometres range expected by Actia. However, tests showed us lower performances in presence of obstacles. Nonetheless, there are restrictions since we cannot use more than 1% of the bandwidth which limited us concerning the frequency at which we sent data. LoRa protocol is also a low consumption protocol which made it interesting for our system which require a high autonomy.
    Concerning the communication between the nodes/gateway and the phones/tablet, we implemented a BLE (Bluetooth Low Energy) connection. Since this was a short-range connection and that phones usually have BLE integrated it seemed to be the best choice.

    GNSS localisation and data processing:

    The localisations periodically sent to the devices were retrieved thanks to a GPS connected to the nodes and gateway. The coordinates retrieved contained degrees and minutes and had to be convert in order to be displayed later in the application. Furthermore, because of the limitations imposed by LoRa protocol we optimised the data sent in a ten bytes dataframe. We formatted the data to include the ID of the sender, the type of message sent (specified by the application) and the coordinates.

    Phone’s and tablet’s applications:

    The phone’s application displays its own location as well as the location of the gateway. It also offers the possibility to send three different type of messages with implemented buttons on the screen. This information is then sent over to the node which changes the type of the message according to the pushed button. This new information will then be sent to the gateway.
    The tablet’s application works in the same way, the only difference being that it displays all the nodes position in addition to its own.

    Energy consumption:

    In this respect, we have chosen to supply the nodes/gateway with a rechargeable LiPo (Lithium Polymer) battery as it has the best performances among all rechargeable batteries. We chose a 3000 mAh battery to supply each node because it is affordable and near to the best performance levels for embedded systems.
    As I am writing this section, we have not finished implementing the nodes. Thus, we do not have any relevant information concerning the consumption of our final product.

    Packaging:

    As our project has a commercial dimension, we have also focused on the packaging part of the deliverables in this proof of concept. Furthermore, we have decided to include an emergency button on the case. The idea is that, in case of problems linked with the tactile interface of the phone that would make it impossible to send messages to the gateway, a physical button could do the job.
