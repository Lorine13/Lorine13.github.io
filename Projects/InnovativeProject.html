Technical Aspects

  • Global description

    The objective of the project “Localisation and Communication” is to provide a tool to make the coordination of a task force in a large outdoor space easier. Our project is more precisely targeting groups composed of one operation’s manager (a chief) and a team of operators. Equipped with an electronic dispositive (called a gateway - GW) and a digital tablet, the chief can determine the precise location of each of his operators on a map. In addition, it is possible for him to send messages and interact either with all of his team or just with one specific operator. As for the operators, they are all equipped with another electronic dispositive (called a node - ND) and a mobile phone with which they can both visualise the chief’s location and send him messages.

    Figure 1: Global representation of the system


    Global specifications:
    • Low cost solution
    • Long battery life, ideally 24-hour autonomy
    • Lightweight (nodes and gateway carried around for hours)
    • 4km range in perfect conditions (line of sight)
    • Minimum five meters accuracy for the GPS

  • System's choices and implementation

    After defining the specifications of the system, we analysed the possible solutions and choose the communication protocol that seemed the more suitable. We conclude that BLE (Bluetooth Low Energy) was the best fit for the communication between the tablet/phones and the nodes, since the connexion is short range, has low power consumption and is already included in the smartphones we would use. For the communication between the nodes and the gateway, we compared different solutions and conclude that LoRa was best. It is long range, free, and shows low energy consumption. We also chose Lopys for the nodes. It was a good match since they are lightweight, cheap, have low energy consumption and can be use for industrial production.
    Another part of the project was the data formatting. When sending a message from the node the gateway or the other way around, it was important for the message sent to be short because of LoRa’s restrictions but also because of the energy consumption. From the GPS coordinates we created a ten bytes message, including the identifier of the node sending the message, an identifier precising the type of message sent (clicked buttons or periodical localisation), and the latitude and longitude of the sending device.

    In the end, our group provided a functional proof of concept allowing one operation’s manager to send and receive messages and positioning from five operators deployed on an outdoor field.
